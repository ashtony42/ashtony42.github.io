Education

"Better schools lead to better jobs and a healthy workforce. All Ohioans stand to benefit from access to educational resources."

Reforming school funding formulas

  • Ohio's education funding formula was ruled unconstitutional in 1997 and despite the Ohio Supreme Court's deadline of one year to replace it, our state legislature has yet to do so.

  • By relying so heavily on property taxes to fund schools, our students' quality of education and successes into adulthood can accurately be predicted based off of their zip code. This is due to the imbalance of funding that arises between higher and lower income school districts.

  • Increasing state funding for public schools would address educational inequities, ensure the overall quality of our kids' educations, and bolster quality of life for undervalued teachers.

Ending school vouchers and public funding of charter schools

  • The ECOT scandal directed $100 million in funding per year from Ohio's public schools into an online charter school that knowingly misreported its attendance numbers.

  • Without a direct set of standards for private schools, the voucher system takes much needed money from school districts which often need it the most and places it into the pockets of for-profit companies and untaxed religious organizations.

Expanding educational access and affordability

  • 1.1 million Ohioans have access to only one or zero high-speed internet providers, and the pandemic has demonstrated that access to the internet is key for the educational process. We must develop infrastructure to ensure that all Ohioans, both urban and rural, can access the internet.

  • The average cost of sending a child to preschool in Ohio is $13,655, which is $1,000 more than the national average for spending per K-12 student. To compare, the average cost of in-state tuition for four-year colleges in Ohio is $16,045. Parents of toddlers are spending almost as much to send their children to school as are parents of college students. Universal Pre-K would put more money into the pockets of working parents and reduce stress associated with childcare affordability.

  • Tuition free technical schools and public colleges would shift Ohio's workforce to become more diverse. This would also help meet demands for higher-paying and skill-specific jobs that require two-year degrees from trade schools.
