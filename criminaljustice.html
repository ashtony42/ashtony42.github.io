Criminal Justice Reform

"Ohio's justice system values punishment over prevention. We deserve a legal code that promotes equitability instead of criminalizing poverty."

Ending the use of capital punishment

  • People of color face disproportionately harsher sentencing than their white counterparts.

  • There is very little evidence to show that the death penalty reduces rates of violent crime.

  • It is estimated that 1 out of 25 people on death row are innocent of the crimes for which they have been convicted. As of Oct. 2020, that works out to be approximately 103 people nationwide.

  • Ohio judges have historically released more people from death row than have gone on to be executed. This causes undue psychological harm to those wrongly convicted and unnecessary strain and costs on our courts.

Ending cash bail and for-profit prisons

  • Cash bail inherently contributes to class inequality within our criminal justice system by imposing harsher punishments on the poor, usually before the accused is even found guilty of a crime.

  • The bail bonds lobby contributes millions of dollars to campaigns against bail reform bills, which are aimed at lessening the economic burdens on both poor offenders and taxpayers.

  • For-profit prisons encourage overinflated rates of incarceration by placing quotas on numbers of inmates within their facilities at a given time.

Decriminalizing marijuana

  • In 2018, 663,000 Americans were arrested on marijuana-related charges. Of those, 609,000 were for possession ALONE. Decriminalization must be paired with blanket expungement for all non-violent offenders' past and current charges.

  • The war on drugs overall has affected black and brown communities at far higher rates than white communities despite nearly exact same rates of drug use between them.

  • States that have legal marijuana industries have been able to appropriate extra funding from marijuana sales taxes into programs that directly benefit their citizens, including schools.

Requiring state licensure for law enforcement

  • The average lawyer takes seven years of higher education to learn the law, while Ohio requires only a high school diploma or GED and less than 800 hours of classroom training and for our police officers.

  • Statistics show that police who hold four year degrees are less likely to face disciplinary action on the job. By requiring a licensure in the areas of human services, social work, or an equivalent social sciences program, we better equip police with the tools needed for successful de-escalation, empathetic service, and the ability to better identify what level of intervention is required for individual situations.

  • Police should not be equipped with the same weapons of war that we supply to our national armed forces. We need demilitarize the police and shift that funding toward incentivizing higher education for law enforcement officials in order to better serve their communities.

Reinforcing programs to strengthen police accountability

  • Information on law enforcement misconduct is considered public in Ohio. We must create an easily accessible and searchable state registry of all police misconduct that does not require a freedom of information request.

  • There is currently no statewide law requiring the use of police body cameras. We must require them and impose disciplinary action on any officer found to have removed or turned theirs off while on the job.

  • To prevent the unlawful violation of Ohioans' rights to free speech and assembly, we must legally define the term rioting and put an end to the senseless arrests of nonviolent protestors.
